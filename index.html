The idea behind CTRL comes from pausing, resetting, and taking back control before a habit becomes something bigger.

The problem

Some habits do not look serious at first.

A lot of students see scrolling, sports betting, and constant phone use as normal. The issue is when those habits start affecting mood, sleep, school, money, or relationships.

01

It starts small

A quick scroll or small bet can slowly become something someone relies on when they are stressed, bored, or trying to escape.

02

The loop is hard to notice

Algorithms, notifications, ads, and betting content can keep pulling users back before they realize how much time or money is being spent.

03

People wait too long

Many students avoid asking for help because they do not want to be judged, labeled, or told they have a serious problem.

How it works

CTRL is built around three simple actions.

The goal is to make the website feel less like a lecture and more like a quick tool students can actually use.

CTRL

Pause

Stop long enough to notice the pattern instead of ignoring it.

ALT

Reflect

Think about what the habit is affecting: mood, money, sleep, school, or relationships.

DEL

Reset

Take one small step to change the pattern or reach out for support.

What CTRL does

A simple place to stop and reflect.

CTRL is not meant to diagnose anyone. It is meant to help someone ask: “Is this still just a habit, or is it starting to control me?”

Learn the warning signs

Simple explanations of gambling and social media patterns.

Take a self-check

An interactive check-in that gives users a moment to reflect.

Find next steps

Basic actions, support ideas, and resources users can look into next.

Designed for

College students who might not realize there is a pattern yet.

Primary persona

The unaware student

Uses social media every day and sometimes bets on sports, but does not think the behavior is serious yet.

Secondary persona

The stressed student

Uses scrolling, betting, or online entertainment as an escape from school, pressure, anxiety, or boredom.

Support persona

The concerned friend

Notices someone acting differently and wants a simple way to understand what might be happening.
